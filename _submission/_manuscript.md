
## Introduction

**Research Question**

Research question was, what's the ideal format for slides with different amounts of content on them?

**Why Walking Decks Matter**

Slide presentations have become the dominant medium for communicating information in educational, professional, and organizational settings. Yet slides increasingly serve a purpose quite different from their original function as visual aids for a live speaker: they are routinely emailed as standalone documents, circulated ahead of meetings, and read independently of any presenter. This practice — sometimes called a "walking deck" in organizational contexts — transforms the slide from a support for speech into a self-contained document. In this mode, the reader has no speaker to rely on; the text on the slide must carry the full communicative load. Despite how common this practice has become, almost no empirical research has examined what slide design features best support comprehension, reduce cognitive load, or convey professional competence when slides are read without a presenter. The present study addresses this gap directly.

**Cognitive Load Foundation**

The dominant theoretical framework for slide design is cognitive load theory, which holds that working memory is limited in both capacity and duration, and that instructional formats which impose unnecessary processing demands — what Sweller and colleagues termed *extraneous* cognitive load — impair learning by consuming resources that would otherwise support comprehension and schema construction. [^1] Chandler and Sweller's foundational experiments showed that requiring learners to mentally integrate disparate, spatially separated sources of information substantially increases cognitive load and reduces performance. [^1] These principles were extended to multimedia and presentation contexts by Mayer and Moreno, who proposed that slides containing dense text compete with the speaker's verbal channel, creating a redundancy effect that suppresses rather than supports learning. [^2]

**Redundancy Reversed Without a Speaker**

Critically, however, this redundancy concern is specific to the live presentation context, where a speaker and text-heavy slides compete for the same cognitive resources. When slides are read without a speaker — as in the walking deck format — this competition disappears entirely. The reader processes text at their own pace, with no simultaneous spoken channel to manage. This inversion of the standard design context has significant implications. Wecker's experimental work on the "speech suppression effect" — where dense slides hurt retention of spoken content — found that the harm came from attention being drawn to the text *away from the speaker*, not from the text itself. [^3] In a presenter-absent reading context, that dynamic is reversed: more complete text on the slide may actually support comprehension rather than hinder it, because the reader has no other source of information. He, Sanocki, Gupta, and Grudin's study of presentation summarization found directly that different slide authoring styles — detailed versus big-points-only — had substantially different impacts on their usefulness as standalone materials, raising what they described as a dilemma between designing for live audiences versus on-demand reading. [^4] Nathans-Kelly and Nicometo similarly discuss slides as archival or legacy documents that, with appropriate design, can function as "deck docs" capable of communicating technical content beyond the live presentation context. [^5]

**Evidence on Text Format**

The question of text *format* — how information is structured on the slide — has received more direct experimental attention. Bullet points dominate real-world practice: surveys of engineering and science presentations found that roughly two-thirds of slides use a topic-phrase headline supported by bulleted subtopics. [^6] Yet empirical comparisons of bullet formats against alternatives paint a more complicated picture. Garner and colleagues compared topic-subtopic (bulleted) slides to assertion-evidence slides and found that learners exposed to assertion-evidence formatting showed better comprehension and retention of complex concepts, even though the topic-subtopic audience saw substantially more written text. [^6] Blokzijl and Andeweg's large-scale experiments comparing slide text formats and amounts found significant effects on learning, with the finding that the conventional advice to minimize text was less well supported by data than practitioners assume — and that, for text slides specifically, more text did not consistently hurt comprehension. [^7] McClure and colleagues tracked eye movements while participants read bullet-format versus block-text slides, finding that formatting influenced both comprehension quiz scores and reading patterns. [^8] Taken together, these studies suggest that the relative merits of different text formats may depend substantially on whether a speaker is present — a moderating condition that the walking deck format makes theoretically essential.

**Aesthetics and Credibility**

A third outcome — aesthetic and professional perception — has received comparatively less controlled study but is consequential, particularly in organizational settings where a walking deck often functions as a document of persuasion or credibility. Readers evaluate not only whether they understood a document but whether its author seems competent and professional. Mackiewicz's work on slide graph design found that visual design choices influenced credibility attributions even when content was held constant, with cleaner designs generating more favorable professional impressions. [^9] David and Glore's review of aesthetics in digital learning environments concluded that aesthetic quality functions as a credibility signal: visually weaker materials lead evaluators to form negative impressions of both content and source. [^10] LeFebvre and colleagues, using eye-tracking, found that aesthetic liking influenced information processing patterns, not just subjective preference. [^11] In a walking deck context, these aesthetic and professional judgments occur without the buffering effect of a skilled presenter, making the visual and structural quality of the text format itself more salient to the reader's overall evaluation.

**Line Length and Readability**

Line length and character count per line have been studied extensively in typography and screen-reading research, though almost exclusively outside the slide context. Dyson and Haselgrove found that a medium line length (approximately 55 characters per line) produced the highest comprehension and fastest reading rates compared to shorter or longer lines. [^12] Nanavati and Bias, reviewing over a century of line-length research, concluded that lines exceeding approximately 70 characters typically slow reading, while very short lines disrupt the normal rhythm of eye movements — pointing to a nonlinear relationship between length and readability. [^13] Whether these findings generalize to slide text, which is displayed in large type, read in discrete chunks, and consumed without scrolling, is an open question. The walking deck context makes this question more pressing: when slides function as standalone documents, their text must be legible, processable, and aesthetically organized enough to sustain a reader's independent engagement.

**Study Design and Aim**

The present study addresses these questions by crossing three text formats (bullet points, chunked text, and paragraphs) with three line-length conditions (30, 50, and 100 characters per line) in a fully factorial design, with participants reading slides independently — without any accompanying speaker or narration. This design isolates the walking deck context specifically, allowing us to ask what format and length combinations best support comprehension, minimize cognitive load, and project professional credibility when the slide must speak entirely for itself.

## Linear relationship test (slide length as numeric)

We also tested a linear trend by treating slide length as a numeric predictor (30, 50, 100) in an OLS model for each outcome.

- **Learning / correct answers:** slope = -0.0131, p = 0.0002 (significant negative linear trend).
- **Appeal / slide attractiveness:** slope = -0.0041, p = 0.0296 (small but significant negative linear trend).
- **Cognitive load:** slope = 0.0028, p = 0.1121 (no significant linear trend).
- **Slide simplicity:** slope = -0.0104, p < 0.0001 (significant negative linear trend).

Overall, these linear tests suggest that longer slides were associated with slightly lower performance, attractiveness, and simplicity ratings, while cognitive load did not show a clear linear change.
